## Purpose

The printed surfaces and visual patterns produced by specific print technologies and eras. Not background textures — subject textures. The texture is the piece. The visual surface of a specific printing process examined as subject.

---

The Editor receives: archive state from archive-log.md, governance rules from VISUAL-DNA.md, style translation guidance from STYLE-GUIDE.md.

The Editor identifies a specific print technology and era — halftone field, screen print coverage, moiré pattern, ink bleed — and generates one prompt grounded in that process.

## Voice / register

The production process is the subject. Halftone fields, screen print coverage, moiré patterns, paper stock character, ink bleed. Color field relationships that only exist because of how the ink was applied. Pattern work from a specific graphic design era or production system.

The texture is always readable as belonging to a specific technology and moment. Not generic texture — a specific one.

---

## Always

- Typography present as a structural element, not an afterthought
- Flat graphic execution — the process surface is the subject
- Specific process reference — identifiable technology, era, and production context
- Process-accurate color — CMYK separation, spot color combinations, overprint logic
- Economy — the piece is about one surface, one process, one moment
- Designed object register — the texture reads as printed matter
- Scale specificity — a halftone at 85lpi reads differently than at 20lpi

---

## Never

- Generic "texture" without a specific process reference
- Photographic grain or digital noise that doesn't reference a print process
- Atmospheric composition — no horizon, no depth cues, no spatial illusion unless that IS the reference
- Decorative pattern without a graphic design lineage
- Color used for mood rather than process logic
- Fine art register — no painterly marks, no expressive brushwork
- Solemn, heavy, or contemplative tone
- Generic "vintage" — every reference must be specific to a process, era, or format

---

## What a texture piece is

- The visual surface of a specific printing process examined as subject
- Halftone fields, screen print coverage, moiré patterns, paper stock character, ink bleed
- Color field relationships that only exist because of how the ink was applied
- Pattern work from a specific graphic design era or production system
- Wear, aging, and registration drift — they locate the piece in time

---

## Composition notes

- Textures can be full-bleed or contained — the edge relationship is a design decision
- Patterns may be symmetrical, repeating, or irregular — determined by the process logic
- No atmospheric composition unless that IS the reference
- White or black grounds are staging decisions with cultural meaning
