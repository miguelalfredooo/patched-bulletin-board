# Module Proposal: Ephemera
## Proposed by: The Editor-In-Chief
## Date: 2026-05-06
## Session: Module development session (post-Issue 001)

---

WHAT IT IS:
Small-format commercial print designed for brief functional life — postage stamps, concert tickets, bus transfers, matchbook covers, trading cards, beer labels, postmarks, receipts — the graphic culture of the object you are meant to throw away.

WHY IT BELONGS:
objects.md is built for the designer's workspace and the finished graphic object at display scale; ephemera operates at a completely different scale and production logic — maximum information compressed into minimum space, designed for immediate legibility and instant disposal, the vernacular print register that objects.md can peripherally touch (stickers, price tags) but was never specified to handle with depth.

EVIDENCE FROM COLLECTION:
Issue 001 produced 13 entries, none from the ephemera register. The archive has no stamps, tickets, matchbooks, trading cards, or receipt-scale print — the gap spans the entire collection so far. The brief explicitly names "social living" and "pop culture" as collection territory; ephemera is where graphic culture meets daily life most directly and most discardably.

EXAMPLE PROMPT:
a 1963 US postage stamp on gummed paper stock, a single color illustration in two-color offset lithography with the denomination in bold sans-serif, the perforated edge showing the mechanical precision of the stamp production process --ar 1:1 --chaos 20 --p m7446277342072143883

NEVER LIST DRAFT:
- Never produce anything at poster, magazine, or large-format scale — the entire composition must fit in a hand
- Never fine art print editions, artist multiples, or gallery objects — always commercial, functional, disposable
- Never "distressed" or "aged" without naming the specific paper stock and production technology that produced the aging
- Never packaging designed to contain a product — that is a different module (packaging reads the shelf; ephemera documents a transaction)
- Never a composition without a clear production-era specific graphic logic — stamps have perforation grids, tickets have serial numbers, matchbooks have strike-surface zones
- Never multiple objects arranged as a collection or specimen display — each prompt is one ephemeral artifact examined alone
