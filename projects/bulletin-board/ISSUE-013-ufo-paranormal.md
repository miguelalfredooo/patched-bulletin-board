## ACT 2 — FULL EDITION

### 1. THE UNKNOWABLE AS SUBJECT

We've spent centuries trying to *resolve* the paranormal. UFO sightings become declassified documents. Ghosts become explainable phenomena. The uncanny gets explained away.

This issue asks a different question: **what if we stopped trying to explain it?**

What if the unknowable—the genuinely inexplicable—became the *subject itself*, not a problem to be solved? Artists and designers working with UFO imagery, paranormal aesthetics, and the deeply strange are abandoning the urge to clarify. They're making work that *lives in uncertainty*. That's the shift.

---

### 2. THE ALIEN AS AESTHETIC

**"Reality Dropout" by Artist Little Thunder**
*BOOOOOOOM | April 10, 2026*

Hong Kong-based artist Little Thunder explores the emotional disconnection between physical presence and digital identity. Her portraits exist between self-presentation and yearning for sincerity—suspended in a space where digital and physical identity fracture.

The work isn't paranormal, but it embodies the paranormal *feeling*: the uncanny gap between how we appear and who we are. In an age of AI, filters, and deepfakes, this gap has become supernatural. We're all a little alien to ourselves now.

**Why it matters for UFO & PARANORMAL:** The alien isn't out there. It's in the mirror. The paranormal is the everyday erosion of certainty about what's real.

---

### 3. ECHOES OF ELSEWHERE

**"Echoes of Elsewhere" by Artist Sylvia Trotter Ewens**
*BOOOOOOOM | April 29, 2026*

Montreal-based painter Sylvia Trotter Ewens navigates memory, identity, and the space between cultures. Born in Honduras, adopted as an infant, she creates fragmented architectural environments that reflect dual belonging: "neither completely of here nor there."

Her work uses photography from Honduras and Québec—two distinct geographies merging into liminal space. The fragments don't resolve into a single narrative. They hover, unfinished, uncanny. This is paranormal in its truest sense: the presence of what isn't there, the haunting of absence.

**Why it matters for UFO & PARANORMAL:** Paranormal aesthetics aren't about ghosts or UFOs. They're about incompleteness, the refusal to resolve, the beauty of not knowing where you belong.

---

### 4. HALFBREED: CONFRONTING THE UNCANNY

**"Halfbreed" by Artist Nahanni McKay**
*BOOOOOOOM | April 20, 2026*

Métis artist Nahanni McKay traces family history through archival documents and landscape photography. She retracesher grandfather's birth certificate—marked with the colonial term "halfbreed"—onto photographs of Regina Beach, Saskatchewan.

The work confronts the bureaucratic labeling of Indigenous identity. It refuses resolution. Identity isn't clarified; it's *documented* in its contradiction. The uncanny here is institutional: the way systems attempt to make legible what can't be reduced to a checkbox.

This is paranormal in the structural sense: the haunting of colonial systems, the presence of what was erased.

**Why it matters for UFO & PARANORMAL:** The paranormal is often structural, not supernatural. Invisible systems that shape identity, belonging, and visibility are more genuinely uncanny than any ghost.

---

### 5. PROCESS AS UNKNOWING

What binds these works isn't subject matter. It's *approach*. Each artist works *toward* uncertainty rather than away from it.

- Little Thunder doesn't resolve the gap between digital and physical; she inhabits it.
- Sylvia Trotter Ewens leaves her fragments unfinished, letting dual geography remain unsynthesized.
- Nahanni McKay doesn't explain Indigenous identity; she documents its bureaucratic haunting.

None of these works offer clarity. That's the point.

---

### 6. THE UNCANNY IN DESIGN

Paranormal aesthetics in design and visual culture often rely on:

- **Negative space** — what's not there is as present as what is
- **Fragmentation** — refusing wholeness, allowing contradiction
- **Ambiguity** — resisting easy reading or resolution
- **Temporal distortion** — the past haunting the present, or vice versa
- **Doubling** — one thing becoming two, identity splitting

These are design tools for working with the genuinely unknowable.

---

### 7. AI AND THE NEW ALIEN

In 2026, the paranormal has shifted. It's not UFOs in the desert anymore (though those still matter). It's:

- Images generated by algorithms that no one fully understands
- Identity fractured across digital platforms
- The uncanny valley of AI-generated faces
- The structural paranoia of surveillance and data
- The alien logic of recommendation systems that know you better than you know yourself

The UFO is the algorithm. The paranormal is the loss of certainty about what's real.

---

### 8. WORKING WITH DREAD

All of these works carry a quality of *dread*. Not fear—something more ambient. A sense that something is off, that resolution isn't coming, that certainty has been suspended.

This is the emotional register of the paranormal. And it's the most honest response to the moment we're in. We don't know what's real. We don't know what we are. And instead of trying to explain it away, these artists are making work that *lives in that space*.

---

### 9. ON THE THEME

**UFO & PARANORMAL** follows **LEGIBLE** (Issue 012) as its shadow. Issue 012 asked: how do we make systems visible? Issue 013 asks: what happens when we *can't* make something visible? What do we do with the genuinely inexplicable?

The answer: we make it beautiful. We let it haunt us. We stop insisting on clarity.

---

### 10. WHAT COMES NEXT

The cultural signal is a shift away from explanation toward *inhabitation*. Artists aren't solving the paranormal. They're living with it, making work that embraces uncertainty as a primary condition.

This is a mature response to a world that has stopped making sense. Not despair, but acceptance. Not confusion, but clarity about confusion itself.

---

### 11. CLOSING

The unknowable is the most honest subject. We don't get to resolve it. All we can do is make something true from the fact that we don't understand. These artists do that. They live in the space between knowing and not knowing, and they make that space visible—or as visible as an invisible thing can be.
