**Art — In Minor Keys**

Koyo Kouoh curated this year's Venice Biennale — titled *In Minor Keys*, opening May 9 — and then did not live to see it open; five of her collaborators carried it forward, preserving her editorial eye intact across more than 100 artists in the Giardini and Arsenale. The theme was already a frequency: the subdominant key, what vibrates beneath the dominant note, what sustains rather than resolves — and the fact of her absence makes the title land differently now, a resonance her collaborators could not have planned.

[The Art Newspaper — Venice Biennale Special 2026](https://www.theartnewspaper.com/2026/05/08/venice-biennale-special-2026podcast)

**Painting — Predicting History: Testing Translation**

Lubaina Himid is showing at the British Pavilion in Venice with *Predicting History: Testing Translation* — paintings that move between text and cloth, personal archive and civic record, asking how much gets lost when one language (visual, cultural, temporal) passes into another. Himid has been making work about what survives translation for four decades; she's still the one asking the question most precisely.

[British Council Venice — Predicting History: Testing Translation](https://venicebiennale.britishcouncil.org/predicting-history-testing-translation)

**Illustration — Echoes of Elsewhere**

Sylvia Trotter Ewens's recent series *Echoes of Elsewhere* — shown on Booooooom — works through layered illustration that refuses clean resolution, each mark sitting on top of earlier ones rather than correcting them, so the finished image carries the whole sequence of its own making. It is illustration as sediment: not the final image but the evidence of every image it passed through to get there.

[Booooooom — Echoes of Elsewhere by Sylvia Trotter Ewens](https://www.booooooom.com/2026/04/29/echoes-of-elsewhere-by-artist-sylvia-trotter-ewens/)

**Sculpture — Elegy (2026)**

Gabrielle Goliath's *Elegy* — commissioned for the South African pavilion, rerouted to a Venice church after the pavilion's cancellation — is a sound installation in which voices perform mourning across multiple channels, grief shaped into frequency rather than image. The space becomes the instrument: stone walls, candlelit air, the particular reverberation of a place built for sound to last longer than usual.

[Gabrielle Goliath — Elegy, Venice 2026](http://elegyinvenice.com/)

**Culture — The Slopification of Political Art**

The Art Newspaper's recent essay names something that's been accumulating: AI-generated political imagery is now the visual language of multiple world governments — Trump's divine iconography, IRGC animated Lego propaganda, protest graphics assembled in minutes — and the effect is a loss of reverberation, images that provoke without resonating, that circulate without landing. The argument is not that speed is wrong but that slop has no frequency; it cannot carry meaning across the gap between the maker and the receiver.

[The Art Newspaper — The Slopification of Political Art](https://www.theartnewspaper.com/2026/04/29/slopification-political-art-press-any-key-to-continue)

**Photography — Walk_**

Derek Beck shoots a single 35mm roll while walking a random route through a city, iPhone in his jacket pocket recording ambient audio simultaneously — fifty rolls over ten years, forty cities, hundreds of strangers — and the project's premise is about frequency: the image and the sound vibrate together, neither quite explaining the other, the gap between them the space where something true lives. He returned to New York this year, revisiting streets he first walked in 2016.

[Booooooom — Walk_ by Photographer Derek Beck](https://www.booooooom.com/2026/05/08/walk_-by-photographer-derek-beck-2/)

**Art History — Preserving the Art of Ed Aulerich-Sugai**

Ed Aulerich-Sugai was a Japanese American artist who died of AIDS in 1987; his partner Daniel Ostrow has spent thirty-eight years keeping his work accessible, and JSTOR Daily this week traced how that stewardship — a love that outlasted a life — became the method by which an artist's frequency continues to travel. The archive is not institutional; it is personal, maintained on a website, sustained by devotion, proof that care is a transmission technology.

[JSTOR Daily — Preserving the Art of Ed Aulerich-Sugai](https://daily.jstor.org/preserving-the-art-of-ed-aulerich-sugai/)

**Opinions — What Carries**

Thom Yorke and Stanley Donwood — who have designed every Radiohead visual since 1994 — opened a joint exhibition in Venice this week, and the question their collaboration poses is the same one it's always posed: what survives the gap between the music and the image, between intention and reception, between making and being made? Their answer, after thirty years, seems to be: the frequency. The specific note the collaborators share. Not the image, not the sound — the resonance between them.

[Artsy — Thom Yorke opens Venice exhibition with Stanley Donwood](https://www.artsy.net/articles)

LensCulture's New York exhibition — thirty-five photographers from eighteen countries, currently on view — is built around a simple editorial premise: a group show of photography *now*, without theme, without argument, just the present moment in its plurality. The show is a curatorial bet on resonance over coherence: that proximity between distinct frequencies produces something that no single image could carry alone.

**Product & Process — The Book Award as Signal**

Booooooom's 2026 Art & Photo Book Award — currently open for submissions — asks artists and photographers to make a book or zine from their work, which is a specific kind of process challenge: not what looks best on screen but what survives the translation to a held object, what resonates at the scale of a page you can close. The book is the format that demands frequency; if it doesn't vibrate in your hands, it doesn't work.

[Booooooom — 2026 Art & Photo Book Award](https://www.booooooom.com/2026/03/26/call-to-submit-2026-booooooom-art-photo-book-award/)

**Visual & Brand — Identity as Frequency, Not Asset**

The LensCulture portrait awards — thirty-nine photographers across multiple categories — reveal something consistent in the strongest work: identity is not declared, it is transmitted. The portraits that hold your attention are the ones where the photographer's frequency and the subject's frequency briefly overlap; the image is the evidence of that meeting. This is what brand design gets wrong when it chases coherence: resonance cannot be mandated, only created by conditions.

[LensCulture — 2026 Portrait Awards](https://www.lensculture.com/photo-competitions/portrait-awards/winners)

**Music & Sound Culture — Arvo Pärt, *Tabula Rasa* (1977)**

Arvo Pärt wrote *Tabula Rasa* in 1977 after eight years of near-total silence — no public compositions, intensive study of Gregorian chant, a search for the minimum — and what he found was the tintinnabuli method: two voices, one moving, one holding a chord tone, the tension between them producing something larger than either. ECM released it in 1984; it has never gone out of print. It is the sonic definition of resonance: what one voice sets in motion in the space between itself and another.

[ECM Records — Arvo Pärt: Tabula Rasa](https://www.ecmrecords.com/catalogue/143502/tabula-rasa-arvo-part)
