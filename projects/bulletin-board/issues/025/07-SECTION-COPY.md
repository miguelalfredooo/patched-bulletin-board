**Scatter Across Cultures: Different Ways of Distributing Meaning**

Western design values density and clarity. Japanese design values scatter and silence. The difference isn't aesthetic—it's cultural. Scatter admits what the West denies: that some things are better left unsaid. Some space is meant to be interpreted, not filled.

In Japanese gardens, every scattered stone has intention. In Chinese calligraphy, the scattered negative space is the composition, not the ink marks. Islamic art uses scatter—geometric repetition and distribution—as spiritual practice. Each culture has a different relationship with how meaning is distributed across space.

Contemporary global design borrows from all of these: the Western grid for structure, the Eastern scatter for soul. The result is hybrid—organized but breathing, structured but alive. Scatter teaches us that different cultures have different ways of organizing meaning. There is no universal grid.
