**Scatter as Accessibility: When Distributed Design Includes Everyone**

A centralized, dense composition excludes people who can't navigate it. But scatter—distributed, spread out—becomes more accessible. Large targets spread across space are easier to reach than small targets clustered together. Scattered information is easier to scan than walls of text.

This is where scatter becomes ethical. Accessibility isn't about diluting design. It's about distribution. Buttons scattered across a page are easier to tap than buttons grouped in menus. Headlines scattered through content are easier to navigate than a dense paragraph. The scatter serves everyone, not just those with optimal vision and fine motor control.

Contemporary accessibility research shows: scattered, generous spacing improves comprehension for everyone—not just people with disabilities. The scatter that helps one person helps everyone. Distribution is fundamentally more inclusive than density.
