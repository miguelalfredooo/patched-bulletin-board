**Photography**

A film poster is a photograph of an idea, not a photograph of the film. Vertigo doesn't show Hitchcock's imagery, the actors, or the plot; it shows an abstraction—geometry, spirals, the sensation of vertigo rendered as visual form. Photographic representation would anchor us in what the film is about; typography and geometry release us to feel what the film might be. The choice to use typography instead of photography is a choice about what to reveal and what to protect. By hiding representation, Bass creates space for interpretation, suggesting: "The experience you'll have is more important than the narrative you'll follow." This restraint as editorial choice demonstrates the power of what we choose not to show.
