**Hands, Clay, Memory: The Objects That Hold Us Together**

The best social objects are made by hand. A potter throwing a bowl. A carpenter shaping wood. A metalworker forging a spoon. These objects carry something factories cannot replicate: the maker's intention. The slight asymmetry that says "I made this for you, not for mass production."

Anthropologists call this material culture. The objects a society makes and keeps reveal who we are. In Japanese tea ceremonies, the most prized bowls are deliberately imperfect—cracked, irregular, humble. Their imperfection proves authenticity. Someone made this. Someone has used this. Someone will pass it to the next generation.

This is the opposite of our disposable moment. Social objects endure. They gather scars—cracks, stains, dents—that prove they've been part of real gatherings. A bread board worn smooth by decades of slicing. A coffee cup with a repaired handle. A table scarred by elbows and wine glasses.

When we invest in handmade, we're not buying nostalgia. We're investing in objects that can actually hold community across time. They become ancestors. They become proof that we belonged to something larger than ourselves.
