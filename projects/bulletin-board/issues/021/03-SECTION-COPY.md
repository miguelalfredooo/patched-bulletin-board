**Goffman's Props: When Objects Become Social Scripts**

Sociologist Erving Goffman argued that all human interaction is performance. We are actors on a stage, and objects are our props. A chair doesn't just support the body—it tells the audience something about the character sitting in it. Throne or stool. Executive desk or shared table.

Designer Don Norman extended this: objects contain *affordances*—visual signals about what we're supposed to do. A round table affords conversation. A high counter affords hierarchy. A low couch affords intimacy. We don't consciously decode these signals. We feel them in our bodies.

The best social objects are transparent about their script. We don't have to think. We just know: this is a place to linger. This is a place to stand. This is a place to confess. The design has already told us who we are supposed to be.

**Sources:**
- Erving Goffman, "The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life"
- Don Norman, "The Design of Everyday Things"
