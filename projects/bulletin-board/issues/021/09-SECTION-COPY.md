**Scale and Authority: When Objects Set the Tone**

A king's table is longer than everyone else's. A judge's bench is higher. An executive's desk is positioned to face the door. These aren't accidents—they're social objects encoding power into form.

Contemporary social design often rejects this hierarchy, but it remains true: the object's proportions determine who leads the conversation. A round table of equal chairs says something different than a rectangular table with a head. A standing-height counter creates different power dynamics than a seated arrangement.

The most honest social objects are transparent about their politics. A communal table with no hierarchy suggests equality. A tiered seating arrangement acknowledges difference. The designer's only responsibility is to choose consciously and make the choice visible, not hidden.
