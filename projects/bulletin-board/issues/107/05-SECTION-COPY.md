**Culture — Decolonization in the Kitchen**

In contemporary Puerto Rican kitchens, something necessary is happening: reclamation. Chefs and home cooks are restoring Taíno names to ingredients, refusing processed substitutes, teaching children the real version. This isn't nostalgia. It's active work. It's the kitchen as a site of decolonization—unlearning the hierarchies imposed during occupation, insisting that the indigenous approach was correct all along. When a chef chooses heirloom plantain over industrial banana, it's not a choice. It's a refusal. The work is urgent and alive. Food security, cultural continuity, linguistic memory—all of it lives in these decisions. The kitchen becomes a small parliament where colonialism is daily overturned, meal by meal, generation by generation.
