# Editorial Brief — Issue 107: PUERTO RICAN CUISINE

**Issue:** 107  
**Theme:** PUERTO RICAN CUISINE — Food as Cultural Memory, Technique, Place, and Resistance  
**Date:** May 9, 2026

---

## Theme Statement

Puerto Rican cuisine is a language—one in which technique is history, flavor is resistance, and every plate holds a story of three continents meeting in a kitchen under colonial occupation.

---

## The 11 Section Angles

### 1. Art
**Kitchens as creative laboratories.** Show how Puerto Rican home cooks and professional chefs approach their work with the same deliberation as painters or sculptors. Focus on the intentionality: how sofrito is built layer by layer, how mofongo's texture is engineered through hand-pressure, how the composition of a plate tells a story. Seek work documenting the hands-on process—the act of cooking as art-making.

### 2. Painting
**Color as flavor memory.** Commission or source paintings that translate the chromatic intensity of Puerto Rican cuisine into abstract visual language. Think: the deep burgundy of mofongo, the golden saffron yellows of arroz con pollo, the charred blacks of tostones. The paintings should feel sensory—not literal depictions of food, but translations of the *sensation* of eating.

### 3. Illustration
**Genealogy and lineage.** Map the cultural ancestry baked into Puerto Rican dishes through illustrated infographics or narrative art. Show the Taíno root (yuca, yams), the African diaspora (okra, rice techniques, frying methods), the Spanish colonial layer (sofrito, stews). Make visible the hidden genealogies—how each dish is a three-way conversation frozen in time.

### 4. Sculpture
**Vessels and tools as cultural objects.** Feature the material culture of Puerto Rican cooking: the pilon (mortar for mashing), the caldero (cooking pot), the colandera (strainer). These aren't just tools—they're ancestors. Commission or showcase sculptural work that honors the weight, the patina, the labor embedded in these objects. How do they shape both the body and the outcome?

### 5. Culture
**Food as decolonization.** Interview contemporary Puerto Rican chefs and home cooks reclaiming indigenous ingredients and techniques. Focus on the active work of unlearning colonial hierarchies in the kitchen: restoring Taíno names, refusing processed substitutes, teaching children the real version. This section should feel urgent and alive—not nostalgic, but necessary.

### 6. Photography
**The table as ceremony.** Document Puerto Rican tables during celebration—not candid, but intentional portraiture of food as ritual. Capture the moment before eating: the arrangement, the hands, the faces. Include close-ups of specific dishes at the moment of completion. The photography should convey both the everyday and the sacred—because in Puerto Rican culture, the ordinary meal is a ceremony.

### 7. Art History
**How empires wrote recipes.** Trace the written documentation of Puerto Rican cuisine through colonial archives, cookbooks, anthropological records. Show how food was documented (or erased) by outside observers. Then contrast with contemporary Puerto Rican recipe writers reclaiming the authority to document their own food. This is about who gets to name and define cuisine.

### 8. Opinions
**Against nostalgia; for continuity.** Commission a short essay that argues against the trap of diaspora food nostalgia—the "abuela's recipe" mythology. Instead, make the case for Puerto Rican cuisine as a *living, changing system*. How do chefs in San Juan differ from chefs in New York from home cooks in Orlando? How does climate change, immigration, economic pressure reshape what's cooked? Food is not frozen; it survives.

### 9. Design & AI Tools
**Mapping flavor systems algorithmically.** Show experimental work exploring Puerto Rican cuisine through data, visualization, or AI—but critically. Don't make it precious. Ask: What does a machine learn about sofrito? Can an algorithm understand terroir? What gets lost and gained when we model food as data? Include both the tools and the skepticism.

### 10. Product & Process
**From seed to table—the supply chain of resistance.** Document a single dish (mofongo, alcapurria, rice and beans) and trace its entire supply chain backward: from the finished plate to the farmer, the distributor, the colonial history that shaped agricultural patterns. What's industrial? What's survived as heirloom? Where are the breaking points in the system?

### 11. Visual & Brand
**The aesthetic of everyday feast.** Design or curate work that shows how Puerto Rican visual culture—from kitchen walls to restaurant signage to home altars—encodes values: color, abundance, joy, survival. This isn't about design trends or contemporary minimalism. It's about how ordinary Puerto Rican visual decisions carry meaning. What does a properly set table communicate?

---

## Visual Direction (Cover)

The cover should feel like **a kitchen coming into focus**—the moment before the first cut, the first taste.

Left side: close-up, extreme detail. A single spoon of sofrito, the garlic just yielding, steam rising. The depth of field so tight that color becomes abstract—deep reds, golds, the micro-landscape of a single ingredient.

Right side: zoom out to a full plate or a table edge—the food in context, surrounded by the hand of the person eating, the worn ceramic, the intimacy of consumption.

The tension between detail and totality—between ingredient and meaning. The fold line between the two should feel like a horizon line, not a break. The type (issue number, theme) should sit underneath, grounded, letting the imagery dominate.

Color palette: Warm. Deep. Earned. No pastels, no Instagram-aesthetic brightness. This should look like what's real in the light of a real kitchen.

---

## Cross-Section Narrative

All 11 sections work in concert to answer one question: **What does it mean to cook under occupation?**

- **Art, Painting, Illustration, Sculpture** establish that Puerto Rican cuisine is a **creative system**—equal to any art form in its sophistication, intentionality, and cultural weight.

- **Culture and Photography** ground that abstraction back in **lived reality**—real tables, real hands, real people celebrating and surviving through food.

- **Art History and Opinions** provide **context and argument**—explaining how we got here historically, and defending Puerto Rican cuisine against the double bind of nostalgia and erasure.

- **Design & AI Tools and Product & Process** ask **uncomfortable questions**: What systems support this cuisine? What gets lost when we measure it? What survives despite the systems working against it?

- **Visual & Brand** closes the loop: showing that **every choice in a Puerto Rican kitchen—visual, material, tactical—is a design decision**. There is no separation between art and cooking, between intention and survival.

The through-line: Puerto Rican cuisine is not past. It's not quaint. It's **active, intelligent resistance made delicious**.

---

## Success Criteria

A perfect Issue 107 will:

1. **Make visible what's usually invisible.** The reader should understand, for the first time, why sofrito matters beyond taste—why it's a technology, a philosophy, a refusal.

2. **Honor without exploiting.** No poverty porn. No "authentic, authentic" caricature. Treat Puerto Rican cuisine as a peer to European haute cuisine, because it is.

3. **Convey complexity.** The issue should make clear that Puerto Rican food is simultaneously everyday and ceremonial, colonial and resistant, historical and living.

4. **Feel urgent.** Not academic. Not nostalgic. This matters *now*—to identity, to decolonization, to the continuity of culture under pressure.

5. **Create an artifact someone will keep.** The design, the writing, the imagery should combine so that this issue becomes a reference—something people return to, gift, use to understand something about Puerto Rican culture they didn't before.

6. **Trust the audience.** No condescension. Assume the reader is as smart as the food is complex. Assume they're ready to think.
