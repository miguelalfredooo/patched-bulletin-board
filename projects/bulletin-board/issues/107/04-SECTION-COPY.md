**Sculpture — Vessels as Ancestors**

The pilon—that heavy mortar for crushing—isn't a tool. It's an ancestor. Wood darkened by decades of pressure, the rim worn smooth by hands, the weight distributed exactly so that mashing plantain requires less force and more knowledge. The caldero carries dents like a history book. The colandera's holes are worn differently depending on who used it last. These aren't romantic objects. They're loaded with labor. A sculptural approach to cooking vessels honors the weight—literal and metaphorical—embedded in them. How does a tool shape not just the outcome, but the body of the cook? How does patina carry memory? When a sculptor approaches these vessels, they're not celebrating nostalgia. They're acknowledging that material objects carry intent, strategy, and the accumulated pressure of survival.
