# Section Guidelines — What Each Section Should Contain

**For Managing Editor:** Use this as a reference while writing each section's art and copy.

---

## The 11 Sections (Locked Structure)

Each Issue has exactly these 11 sections. The theme is customized, but the section names are always the same.

---

## Section 1: Art

**Purpose:** Opens the Issue. Sets the cultural tone.

**Copy Guidelines:**
- Lead with something visual or experiential
- Introduce the theme indirectly through an art or design lens
- 2-4 sentences, 60-120 words
- Tone: Warm, inviting, sets stage for what's coming

**Art Guidelines:**
- Distinctive visual that signals "this is about design/culture"
- Can be abstract or representational
- Should feel fresh (not generic)

**Example Copy:**
"Design as discovery. What happens when you look closely at what's already here? This Issue explores how attention reveals the hidden: visual patterns, cultural movements, processes we never noticed."

**Sources:** 1-2 design/art publications

---

## Section 2: Painting

**Purpose:** Explores visual art tradition and contemporary painting.

**Copy Guidelines:**
- Can be historical or contemporary
- Focus on how painting thinks about the theme
- 2-4 sentences, 60-120 words
- Tone: Authoritative but accessible

**Art Guidelines:**
- Visual representation of the painting angle
- Can reference actual paintings or abstract representation

**Example Copy:**
"Painting has always been about what the eye sees and the hand records. Contemporary painters are asking: what happens when you paint something you've never looked at before? When observation becomes obsession?"

**Sources:** 1-2 art/painting references

---

## Section 3: Illustration

**Purpose:** Explores illustration and drawn imagery.

**Copy Guidelines:**
- Different from painting—illustration is communication, not just expression
- How illustration translates thought to image
- 2-4 sentences, 60-120 words
- Tone: Specific, reference actual illustrators/styles

**Art Guidelines:**
- Visual showing illustration aesthetic
- Can be decorative or narrative

**Example Copy:**
"Illustration is the art of translation: taking an idea and making it visible. The best illustrators don't just draw—they reveal. They show us what we didn't know we were looking at."

**Sources:** 1-2 illustration/visual design references

---

## Section 4: Sculpture

**Purpose:** Explores three-dimensional form and space.

**Copy Guidelines:**
- Move beyond 2D—how does 3D change the experience?
- Space, materiality, embodiment
- 2-4 sentences, 60-120 words
- Tone: Tactile, physical

**Art Guidelines:**
- Visual evoking three-dimensionality
- Can be abstract or representational

**Example Copy:**
"Sculpture forces you to walk around it. To experience it from different angles. To see how light changes it. How material ages it. How space transforms it. Every view is different."

**Sources:** 1-2 sculpture/3D design references

---

## Section 5: Culture

**Purpose:** Explores cultural movements, identity, society.

**Copy Guidelines:**
- Broader than art—social, political, cultural context
- How does the theme show up in culture at large?
- 2-4 sentences, 60-120 words
- Tone: Observational, specific

**Art Guidelines:**
- Visual representing cultural phenomenon
- Can be documentary or artistic interpretation

**Example Copy:**
"Culture is how we make meaning together. Right now, we're discovering that what was hidden—voices, stories, perspectives—were always there. We just weren't looking."

**Sources:** 1-2 cultural/societal references

---

## Section 6: Photography

**Purpose:** Explores photographic practice and the image.

**Copy Guidelines:**
- Photography as act of looking and choosing
- The eye of the photographer
- 2-4 sentences, 60-120 words
- Tone: Visual, observant

**Art Guidelines:**
- Visual evoking photographic sensibility
- Can be abstract or realistic

**Example Copy:**
"Photography is the art of framing. What you choose to include—and exclude—makes the meaning. Every photograph is a discovery: this moment, this light, this angle, that no one else will capture exactly this way."

**Sources:** 1-2 photography references

---

## Section 7: Art History

**Purpose:** Provides historical or theoretical perspective.

**Copy Guidelines:**
- Can be deep historical context or contemporary theory
- How does the past inform the theme?
- 2-4 sentences, 60-120 words
- Tone: Scholarly but accessible

**Art Guidelines:**
- Visual suggesting history/continuity
- Can reference historical movements or contemporary theory

**Example Copy:**
"Art history teaches us that discovery is cyclical. Movements that seemed buried resurface. Techniques we thought forgotten are reinvented. Every generation discovers what was always there."

**Sources:** 1-2 historical/theoretical references

---

## Section 8: Opinions

**Purpose:** Critical takes, essays, arguments.

**Copy Guidelines:**
- Opinion-driven, not just factual
- Take a stance on the theme
- 2-4 sentences, 60-120 words
- Tone: Provocative, thoughtful

**Art Guidelines:**
- Visual that suggests debate or multiple perspectives
- Can be confrontational or introspective

**Example Copy:**
"We pretend we're discovering new things. We're mostly rediscovering what was always hidden in plain sight. The question isn't whether it's there. It's whether we're ready to see it."

**Sources:** 1-2 opinion/essay pieces

---

## Section 9: Design & AI Tools

**Purpose:** Design tools, technology, AI culture.

**Copy Guidelines:**
- How tools change the way we work and think
- AI not as standalone topic—as tool/amplifier
- 2-4 sentences, 60-120 words
- Tone: Practical, forward-thinking

**Art Guidelines:**
- Visual suggesting tools, technology, process
- Can be abstract or representational

**Example Copy:**
"New tools reveal new possibilities. AI isn't replacing discovery—it's accelerating it. We're finding patterns in data that humans could never see. The question: what do we do with what we find?"

**Sources:** 1-2 design/tool/AI references

---

## Section 10: Product & Process

**Purpose:** How things are made, workflows, craft.

**Copy Guidelines:**
- Behind-the-scenes, process-focused
- What does the making reveal?
- 2-4 sentences, 60-120 words
- Tone: Insider knowledge, specific

**Art Guidelines:**
- Visual showing process, making, craft
- Can be procedural or conceptual

**Example Copy:**
"The best makers know something the rest of us don't: the process reveals the product. You can't fake craft. It shows in every detail, every choice, every constraint."

**Sources:** 1-2 craft/process/maker references

---

## Section 11: Visual & Brand

**Purpose:** Graphic design, visual systems, branding.

**Copy Guidelines:**
- How visual identity communicates
- The power of consistent design
- 2-4 sentences, 60-120 words
- Tone: Strategic, visual

**Art Guidelines:**
- Visual showing brand/identity/system thinking
- Can be logo-focused or system-focused

**Example Copy:**
"Every brand is a visual discovery. A mark that says: I exist. I have a point of view. I'm consistent. I can be trusted. The best brands don't shout. They simply appear, and you know exactly who they are."

**Sources:** 1-2 branding/identity references

---

## Universal Rules (All Sections)

✅ **DO:**
- Reference actual artists, works, publications (be specific)
- Connect to the theme indirectly (felt, not stated)
- Write in second person or imperative occasionally ("Ask yourself," "Notice how")
- Use active voice
- Include 1-2 source links

❌ **DON'T:**
- Use filler or padding
- State the theme explicitly
- Be too academic or abstract
- Use jargon without explanation
- Repeat what other sections say

---

## Word Count

**Target:** 60-120 words per section

**Too short (<60):** Add another sentence or two, give more detail

**Too long (>120):** Cut to essentials, remove examples that don't add

**Tool:** `python validate.py issues/[number]` checks all word counts

---

## Sources

Each section should have 1-2 sources:
- Real URLs (not made up)
- Relevant to the section's topic
- Accessible (not paywalled)
- Recent (within last year, generally)

---

## Tone Consistency

All 11 sections should sound like they're from the same magazine, written by the same editorial voice. Individual personality is good—but consistency matters.

Check: Do all sections feel like they belong together?

---

## How to Use This Guide

1. Read the section you're writing
2. Review the purpose and guidelines
3. Look at the example copy
4. Write your own (don't copy the example)
5. Check: Does it fit the purpose?
6. Check: Is it 60-120 words?
7. Check: Are sources real and relevant?
8. Done—move to next section
