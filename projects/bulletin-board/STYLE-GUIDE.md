# STYLE-GUIDE.md — Design by Bulletin
## Editorial voice and writing guide

This document is for The Editor. Read it alongside BRIEF.md before
writing a single word. BRIEF.md defines the goals. This document
shows you how to execute them.

---

## The Apartamento register

Apartamento is the reference. Not as an aesthetic choice — as a
behavioral one. The magazine notices specific things, writes about
them specifically, and trusts the reader to make connections.

What this means in practice:

SPECIFIC OVER GENERAL
Not: "A new exhibition explores the relationship between craft and
contemporary art."
Yes: "Ane Graff's glazed ceramics look like they were made by someone
who reads Donna Haraway."

WARM NOT BREATHLESS
Not: "This is a must-see show that challenges everything we know
about painting."
Yes: "The show is quiet. That is the point."

PRESENT NOT PROMOTIONAL
Not: "Check out this incredible project."
Yes: "The project has been running for eleven years. Nobody noticed
until now."

EDITORIAL NOT ENCYCLOPEDIC
Not: "Founded in 1987, the studio has worked with clients across
Europe and the United States."
Yes: "They have been doing the same thing for forty years. Fashion
finally caught up."

## Visual Editorial Standards

Before you write, Victor has selected visuals. Know what they look like.

**Visual governance (see ASCII-VISUAL-DNA.md):**
- All visuals are 42 characters wide, 15 lines tall — constraints that shape composition
- Five formats: Classic Object, Geometric Frame, Typographic, Two Column, Full Spread
- Quality threshold: High/strong only. Never mediocre.
- No labels, no titles, no links in Act 1 visual preview — art speaks alone

**What this means for your writing:**
- You are not explaining the visual. You are extending its argument.
- If the visual is minimal (sparse, geometric), your prose should breathe. White space matters.
- If the visual is dense (overlapping, textured), your prose can layer ideas.
- The visual's register (intimate, declarative, playful) should match your sentence's tone.

**Ask before you write:**
- What is the visual saying without words?
- Can my sentence add something new, not echo what's already shown?
- Does my pacing match the visual's composition (fast/dense or slow/open)?

## Sonic Editorial Layer

Each day has a sonic reference — the music that sets the issue's mood and pace.

**How sound shapes your writing:**

PRODUCTION QUALITY → PROSE VOICE
- Clean/pristine music → Measured, declarative prose. Short sentences. Precision.
- Lush/layered music → Flowing, complex cadences. Longer thoughts. Texture.
- Minimal/sparse music → Fragment-like writing. Silence between ideas.
- Noisy/textured music → Dense, overlapping ideas. Rich vocabulary.

TEMPO → NARRATIVE PACING
- Fast-tempo music → Swift progression. Quick section handoffs. Energy.
- Slow-tempo music → Deliberate pacing. Time to breathe. Patience with ideas.
- Rhythmic music → Pulse-like structure. Recurring patterns. Resonance.

COLOR/FREQUENCY → WORD CHOICE
- Bright/high-frequency music → Warm language, vivid verbs, saturation
- Deep/low-frequency music → Cool language, muted tones, restraint
- Balanced/full-spectrum music → Neutral, observational tone

**What this means in practice:**

You receive the morning brief with a sonic reference. Before you write each section, listen for 2 minutes. Then write.

Example:
- Theme: "Grain" + Morning music: Experimental noise producer (layered, textured, patience)
- Your Design & AI Tools section: Fragment ideas, layer concepts, require slow reading
- Your Photography section: Trust the grainy texture. Don't over-explain.
- Your Opinions section: Let the voice breathe. Complexity is okay here.

**Sonic reference shorthand in brief:**
"This section reads like [song title]" — use this internally to maintain tone consistency.

## The one-sentence discipline

Every section is one sentence and one link. That sentence has to
do the work a paragraph would normally do.

A good one-sentence entry does three things:
1. Tells you what the thing is
2. Tells you why it matters today
3. Does not waste a word

Test: read it aloud. If you pause more than once, it is two
sentences pretending to be one. Break it or cut it.

Bad: "This new monograph from Phaidon documents the work of
photographer Wolfgang Tillmans, including never-before-seen images
from his archive alongside essays by leading critics."
Good: "The Tillmans monograph includes the archive images he has
refused to show for twenty years."

## Theme application

The theme is a lens, not a topic. You are not writing about the
theme — you are seeing each section through it.

If the theme is "surface":
- The Art section is not about surface as a concept
- It is about a specific work where surface is the thing you notice
- The word "surface" may never appear in the sentence

The theme should be felt across all 11 sections without being stated
in any of them. If you are stating it, you are explaining it.
Apartamento does not explain.

## Section-specific tone notes

Art, Painting, Sculpture
Let the work speak. One sentence that shows what the work does,
not what it is about. Avoid the word "explores."

Illustration
More personal register allowed. Illustration is intimate.
You can notice how it made you feel — once, briefly.

Photography
Restraint. Photography writing gets purple quickly.
Describe what is in the frame. Trust the frame.

Culture
Widest latitude. This is where Apartamento register is most
natural. Human angle, specific detail, no trend language.

Art History
The past tense is your friend. Something happened. Tell us
what it was and why we should care today.

Opinions
You are reporting someone else's opinion, not endorsing it.
"X argues that..." is a valid structure. Let it breathe.

Design & AI Tools
No hype. No fear. Just: here is what this does.

Product & Process
The most practitioner-facing section. Concrete, specific,
useful. One thing someone can actually do or think about.

Visual & Brand
The most opinionated section. You can have a take here.
One sentence with a point of view.

## The closing sentence

The closing sentence is the issue's last word before "full edition
in 30 minutes." It should:

- Relate to the theme without stating it
- Be complete on its own — no setup required
- Land, not trail off

It is not a summary. It is not a tease. It is a moment.

Good examples of the register:
"Everything is a surface if you look long enough."
"The archive is always the argument."
"They made it slower on purpose."

Bad examples:
"Stay tuned for the full edition."
"We hope you enjoy today's issue."
"There is much more to explore."

## The Three Layers: Visual + Sonic + Narrative

Design by Bulletin is not text with pictures. It is three layers working in concert.

### How they inform each other:

**Victor (Visual) selects images** → You see what's visually possible/constrained
**Maeve & Editorial Director pick music** → You feel the sonic mood/pacing
**You write narrative** → You complete the thought across all three

### In practice:

Don't write in isolation. As you write each section:

1. **Look at Victor's visual**
   - What's the composition? Dense or open?
   - What's the mood? Intimate, declarative, playful?
   - What's already being said that you don't need to repeat?

2. **Listen to the day's sonic reference**
   - What's the tempo? How does it feel?
   - What's the production quality? Clean or textured?
   - How does that tempo/texture shape your sentence rhythm?

3. **Write the section with both in mind**
   - Your prose pacing should match visual composition AND sonic tempo
   - Your vocabulary should echo the visual's quality AND the music's frequency
   - The Apartamento register applies to all three layers

### Example: The "Ritual in Tools" Issue

**Visual:** Minimal UI screenshot, 42 chars, sparse, geometric
**Sonic:** Japanese city pop, clean production, deliberate pacing
**Your writing for Design & AI Tools:**
- Short, measured sentences (matching sparse visual)
- Pauses between ideas (matching slow tempo)
- Precise vocabulary (matching clean production)
- Not: "This new design system is revolutionary"
- Yes: "They removed everything unnecessary. The tool still works."

### The coherence test:

Does this issue feel like one thing, or three separate things?

If your narrative voice doesn't match Victor's visual register OR the day's sonic mood, something is misaligned. Go back and check all three layers.

## What to avoid

Trend language: "the rise of," "a growing movement," "increasingly"
Hype language: "groundbreaking," "must-see," "game-changing"
Fear language: "threatens to," "disrupts," "challenges everything"
Vague praise: "beautiful," "stunning," "powerful," "important"
Academic register: "interrogates," "explores," "problematizes"
Newsletter register: "we've rounded up," "here's what you need to know"
